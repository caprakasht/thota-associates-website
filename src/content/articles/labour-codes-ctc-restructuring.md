Most of the CTC structures we have audited since November 2025 look compliant on paper and are not. The headline rule — wages must be at least 50% of total remuneration — has been widely reported. The architecture of the rule, and the trap most employers walk into while trying to comply with it, has not.

This article is for the CFO, HR head or founder who has read the headlines, asked their payroll team to "make sure we're at 50% basic," and quietly assumed the matter is closed. It is not closed. Here is what we actually look at when we audit a structure.

## Section 2(y) is built in three parts, not one

The definition of *wages* in Section 2(y) of the Code on Wages, 2019 — reproduced identically in the three other Codes — has three operative parts that must be read together.

**Part 1 — the inclusive limb.** Wages means "all remuneration… and includes" basic pay, dearness allowance and retaining allowance. The opening clause is sweeping: every payment to an employee is presumed to be remuneration unless something specifically takes it out.

**Part 2 — the exclusion list.** Eleven specific items are taken out: statutory bonus, house accommodation in kind, employer PF, conveyance allowance or travelling concession, sums to defray special expenses, HRA, award/settlement payments, overtime, commission, gratuity, and retrenchment compensation. This list is exhaustive. Anything not in it falls back into the inclusive limb above.

**Part 3 — the 50% proviso.** If payments under clauses (a) to (i) exceed half of total remuneration, the excess is deemed wages. Note the range: (a) to (i) only. Gratuity and retrenchment compensation are ring-fenced and don't count toward the 50% threshold.

## The trap: when 50% quietly becomes 60%

Consider a structure that looks textbook: Basic + DA at exactly 50%, defined exclusions at 40%, a Special Allowance of 10% as a balancing residual. The employer reads this as "wages 50%, exclusions 40% — well within the cap."

In our view, the law reads it differently. A "Special Allowance" is not in the inclusive limb. It is also not in the exclusion list. It therefore falls into the main body of the inclusive limb and is wages by default. The Supreme Court reached the same conclusion before the Code was drafted, in *RPFC (II) West Bengal v. Vivekananda Vidyamandir, 2019*, holding that allowances paid universally and without contingency are part of basic wages for PF.

So the wage base for PF, gratuity and bonus is not 50% of CTC. It is 60%.

| Component | Employer's reading | What Section 2(y) does |
|-----------|--------------------|------------------------|
| Basic + DA | 50% (wages) | 50% (wages) |
| Defined exclusions (a)–(i) | 40% (excluded) | 40% (excluded) |
| Special Allowance | 10% (excluded?) | **10% (deemed wages)** |
| Effective wage base | **50%** | **60%** |

The 50% proviso is not even triggered — defined exclusions are at 40%, comfortably below the cap. The Special Allowance bypasses the proviso entirely and goes in through the front door. PF and gratuity arrears with interest run from 21 November 2025.

## A worked example: ₹12 lakh CTC

Take a software engineer on ₹12 lakh annual CTC. Old structure: 30% basic, the rest in HRA, Special Allowance and "Other." New structure must satisfy two tests, applied together.

| | Pre–Nov 2025 | Post–Nov 2025 (compliant) |
|---|---|---|
| Basic + DA | ₹3,60,000 | ₹6,00,000 |
| HRA | ₹1,80,000 | ₹3,00,000 |
| Conveyance / Medical (against bills) | — | ₹1,20,000 |
| Other / Special Allowance | ₹6,60,000 | nil |
| LTA | — | ₹1,08,000 |
| Employer PF (12%) | ₹43,200 | ₹72,000 |
| Wage base for gratuity | ₹3,60,000 | ₹6,00,000 |

Basic nearly doubles. Employer PF rises by ₹28,800 a year. Gratuity wage base nearly doubles. Monthly take-home falls because more of the same CTC is now flowing into PF instead of the bank account. Total CTC is unchanged.

## Two tests every CTC must pass

We use these as the working frame for every audit:

- **Mapping test.** Every component that is not Basic, DA or Retaining Allowance must be mapped to a specific exclusion clause from (a) to (k), and must genuinely fit that clause in substance — not merely in label. Conveyance must defray actual conveyance, ideally against bills. Medical reimbursement must be against medical bills. Generic balancing figures fail this test.
- **Proviso test.** Even after correct mapping, the aggregate of clauses (a) to (i) must not exceed 50% of total remuneration.

A structure that passes the headline "50% basic" check but fails Test 1 — through a Special Allowance, an Other Allowance, or any unmapped residual — is non-compliant from day one.

## LTA: the soft spot worth flagging

Clause (d) excludes "any conveyance allowance or the value of any travelling concession." Whether LTA fits the second phrase is open. Pro-exclusion: LTA is, textually, a travelling concession. Anti-exclusion: the phrase historically referred to free or subsidised travel passes, and LTA is a distinct tax-exempt allowance under Section 10(5) of the Income Tax Act that the drafters could have named explicitly. No Ministry FAQ resolves it. No judicial pronouncement under the new Code exists.

We read LTA as defensible at roughly 50–60% confidence. The defensive response is not to abandon LTA — it remains a legitimate component — but to size it modestly, not as the residual that absorbs whatever doesn't fit elsewhere.

## What employers should actually do

In rough order of urgency:

1. **Audit every active salary structure.** Identify employees where Basic + DA is below 50% of CTC, and separately, employees whose structure includes a Special Allowance, Other Allowance or unmapped residual of any size. The second category often surprises people more than the first.
2. **Pick a structuring philosophy and apply it consistently.** Either run wages exactly at the floor (lowest take-home dip, no buffer) or run a 53–55% wage base with a deliberate buffer against minor variations and the LTA question. We generally recommend the second for organisations with multi-state operations or any inspection-regime exposure.
3. **Reconfigure payroll for 48-hour full-and-final settlement.** The Codes mandate F&F within two days of cessation. Most payroll systems are built around 30–45 day settlement cycles. Run a parallel cycle for one month before going live.
4. **Issue revised appointment letters.** Mandatory under the Codes. Without one, an employee's claimed terms of employment carry the day in a dispute.
5. **Brief your people in plain English before the next payroll runs.** This is where most restructurings go wrong. The take-home dip lands in the bank account before the explanation lands in the inbox, and goodwill evaporates.

## How to brief the team

Keep it short and honest. Three sentences usually do it.

Your CTC has not changed. A larger share now flows into your provident fund and gratuity entitlement instead of monthly take-home — this is required by the new Labour Codes effective from 21 November 2025. Monthly net pay falls by a few thousand rupees; long-term retirement and exit benefits compound to materially more over a career.

What employees most often want to know after that: *will gratuity at exit actually be larger?* (yes, because it is computed on last-drawn basic, which is now higher); *can I opt out?* (no — it is statutory); *does this apply to capped-PF employees?* (the monthly impact is smaller, but the gratuity uplift is the same).

## Bottom line

If I were sitting across a coffee table from a client today, I would say this: the 50% rule is a floor, not a ceiling, and almost every structure I have seen this quarter was designed against the wrong reading of it. The fix is not difficult, but it has to be done before the next inspection cycle, and it has to be communicated to employees before they see the dip in their bank account. Sequence matters as much as the structure itself.
